The idea

A draw is a price problem — but the price isn’t only this bet

Counting outs tells you how often you hit. The decision is whether the money going in is worth it, and that depends on three things: the price the pot is laying you, the equity your draw has, and how much stack is behind. The last one is what most quick pot-odds math ignores — and it’s the difference between a call that prints and a call that bleeds.

Step 1 — the price

Pot odds: the equity you need to break even

need = amount to call ÷ (pot after you call)

Pot 100, villain bets 50: you call 50 to win the 150 out there. After your call the pot is 200 and you put in 50 of it — 50 ÷ 200 = 25%. Bigger bets demand more equity; memorise the common sizes on the drill panel.

Step 2 — the equity, and how many cards you actually buy

All-in vs. money behind

  • All-in (or on the river): there’s no more betting, so you see every remaining card. On the flop that’s two cards — outs × 4. On the turn it’s one — outs × 2. Compare straight to the price; there are no implied or reverse-implied odds because the hand is over.
  • Money behind on the flop: calling does not buy you the river. Villain can bet again on the turn and price you off, so this call only buys the turn card — value it with one card, outs × 2. That number is usually below the price a flop bet lays you, which is why so many draws are not a pure-odds call.

Step 3 — implied odds: does the stack justify it?

What you must win later to make a short-priced draw pay

extra needed = call × (1 − e₁) ÷ e₁ − (pot + bet)

When one-card equity (e₁) doesn’t cover the price, the rest has to come from the chips you win on later streets when you hit. If that “extra needed” is more than the effective stack behind, the implied odds aren’t there — fold. If the stack behind covers it, the draw is justified — call. Deep stacks make more draws callable; short stacks make speculative draws (gutshots, bare overcards) folds. Remember you won’t stack villain every time, so shade the marginal ones toward folding.

The other direction — reverse implied odds

When hitting still loses

A draw to a second-best hand — a flush without the ace (a bigger flush is possible), or two overcards that can pair into a worse hand than villain already holds — wins less often than it completes. The fix is to discount your outs: treat a non-nut flush draw as ~7 outs instead of 9, two overcards as ~4 instead of 6. This drill shows both the raw outs and the clean count, and grades on the clean count. The cleaner your draw, the more aggressively you can chase it.
