The idea

A draw is a price problem, not a card problem

Counting outs tells you how often you’ll hit. It doesn’t tell you whether to put money in. To decide that you compare two percentages: the equity the pot is offering you (your pot odds) against the equity your draw actually has. If you have more than you need, calling prints money over the long run; if you have less, folding does. This drill makes that comparison automatic.

Step 1 — the price

Pot odds: the equity you need to break even

need = amount to call ÷ (pot after you call)

If the pot is 100 and villain bets 50, you call 50 to win the 150 already out there. After your call the pot is 200, and you put in 50 of it: 50 ÷ 200 = 25%. You need to win at least one time in four to break even. Bet size is all that matters here — bigger bets demand more equity. Memorise the common ones in the table on the drill screen.

Step 2 — the equity

Rule of 2 and 4: outs into a percentage

flop (two cards to come): outs × 4  ·  turn (one card): outs × 2
  • On the flop, with two cards still to come, multiply your outs by 4. Nine outs (a flush draw) ≈ 36%.
  • On the turn, with only the river left, multiply by 2. The same flush draw is now ≈ 18%.
  • Past eight outs the ×4 over-counts. Subtract one point for each out above 8: a 15-out monster is ≈ 15×4 − 7 = 53%, not 60%. The drill grades against the exact number but accepts the rule estimate.

Step 3 — the call

Compare, then commit

Have > need → call. Have < need → fold. That’s the whole decision. The drill also shows the EV in chips of calling so you can see how much each correct fold saves and each correct call earns. When the two numbers are within a point or two of each other the spot is break-even — either play is fine, and the drill marks both correct.

One caveat the pure math ignores: implied odds. If you can win more on later streets when you hit, you can call slightly worse than your raw pot odds — and with reverse implied odds (a draw to a second-best hand) you need a little more. This trainer drills the baseline; adjust from there at the table.
