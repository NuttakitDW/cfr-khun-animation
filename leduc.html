Rules

Leduc Hold'em — what's different from Kuhn

Deck

6 cards: 2× J, 2× Q, 2× K. Each player gets 1 private card; later 1 community card is dealt from the rest.

Round 1 (pre-board)

Both ante 1 chip. Players take turns: check, bet (2 chips), call, raise (+2 more), or fold. Max 2 raises per round.

Community card

After round 1 ends (without a fold), one card from the remaining deck is revealed. This shifts each player's hand strength.

Round 2 (post-board)

Same betting structure but 4-chip bets. Again capped at 2 raises.

Showdown

If your private card matches the community card, you have a pair and win. Otherwise: higher private card wins.

Why this is harder

288 info sets vs Kuhn's 12. Strategy must reason about pairs, bluffing across two streets, and pot-odds at varying bet sizes. Convergence takes longer iterations.

How Exploitability is Computed

Topological dynamic programming for the best response

Exploitability measures how much chip equity an opponent could win against your strategy by playing a best response. At Nash equilibrium no deviation helps either side, so exploitability = 0. Reported here in milli-big-blinds per game (mbb/g) — the standard poker AI metric (1 mbb/g = 0.001 chips lost per hand against a perfect opponent).

ε(σ) = ½ · ( BR_value(P1, σ̄_P2) + BR_value(P2, σ̄_P1) )

Where BR_value(player, opp_strategy) is what player achieves with their best response to a fixed opponent strategy. In a zero-sum game the Nash values cancel, so ε ≥ 0 always; ε = 0 only at Nash. Both BRs are computed against the average strategy σ̄.

The challenge in Leduc: Each player has 144 information sets with 2 or 3 actions each. Naive brute-force enumeration of pure best-response strategies needs ~3¹⁴⁴ ≈ 10⁶⁸ candidates. Infeasible.

(For Kuhn, with only 64 pure strategies per player, we just enumerate them all. Leduc demands a smarter algorithm.)

Topological DP approach — four steps:

1. Order brPlayer's info sets deepest-first. Round 2 before round 1; within a round, longer histories before shorter (e.g., for P1 in round 2: "rr" and "cr" before ""). This ordering guarantees that whenever we evaluate a subtree, every brPlayer decision inside that subtree has already been committed.

2. Compute counterfactual value per action at each info set in order:

CFV(I, a) = Σ over consistent deals d of: P(d) · opp_reach(I; d) · V_subtree(after a; d)

where consistent deals are the chance outcomes where brPlayer's card matches I (and, in round 2, the board card matches), and opp_reach is the product of the opponent's σ̄ probabilities along their actions in I's history (brPlayer's contribution is treated as 1 — the "counterfactual" assumption). The subtree evaluator uses already-decided BR at deeper brPlayer info sets and σ̄ at opponent info sets.

3. Set BR(I) = argmax_a CFV(I, a). One pure action per info set. Lock it in brStrat[I].

4. Compute total expected utility under BR with one final tree walk: sum over all chance outcomes weighted by P(d), evaluating the subtree using BR at brPlayer nodes and σ̄ at opponent nodes.

Cost: ~144 info sets × ~30 deals × short subtree walks ≈ a few hundred thousand operations per BR. About 50–200 ms total for both BRs on a typical machine — too slow to run every iteration, but fast enough to refresh every few seconds. The visualization throttles it adaptively: spending ~10% of refresh time on this metric.

Why topological order matters: When we evaluate the subtree for action a at info set I, that subtree may contain other brPlayer info sets. Their BR must already be decided, otherwise we'd recurse into an undefined choice. Processing deepest-first guarantees this — like solving a feed-forward network from outputs back to inputs.
